# Chord Theory and Harmonic Relationships

Understanding chord structures helps interpret consonance/dissonance patterns in spectral analysis. Chords are built from interval relationships, which manifest as specific frequency ratios.

## Major Chords

### Major Triad
- **Formula:** 1-3-5
- **Example:** C-E-G
- **Suffix:** C
- **Frequency Ratios:** Root, Major Third (5:4), Perfect Fifth (3:2)
- **Character:** Stable, consonant, resolved

### Fifth (Power Chord)
- **Formula:** 1-5
- **Example:** C-G
- **Suffix:** C5
- **Frequency Ratios:** Root, Perfect Fifth (3:2)
- **Character:** Very stable, neutral (no major/minor)

### Suspended Fourth
- **Formula:** 1-4-5
- **Example:** C-F-G
- **Suffix:** Csus4
- **Character:** Tension, wants to resolve to major

### Suspended Second
- **Formula:** 1-2-5
- **Example:** C-D-G
- **Suffix:** Csus2
- **Character:** Open, ambiguous

### Added Ninth
- **Formula:** 1-3-5-9
- **Example:** C-E-G-D
- **Suffix:** C(add9)
- **Character:** Rich, colorful extension

### Sixth
- **Formula:** 1-3-5-6
- **Example:** C-E-G-A
- **Suffix:** C6
- **Character:** Jazz/pop color, stable

### Sixth, Added Ninth
- **Formula:** 1-3-5-6-9
- **Example:** C-E-G-A-D
- **Suffix:** C6/9
- **Character:** Lush, sophisticated

### Major Seventh
- **Formula:** 1-3-5-7
- **Example:** C-E-G-B
- **Suffix:** Cmaj7
- **Character:** Dreamy, jazzy, sophisticated

### Major Ninth
- **Formula:** 1-3-5-7-9
- **Example:** C-E-G-B-D
- **Suffix:** Cmaj9
- **Character:** Very lush, extended harmony

### Major Seventh, Sharp Eleventh
- **Formula:** 1-3-5-7-#11
- **Example:** C-E-G-B-F#
- **Suffix:** Cmaj7#11
- **Character:** Lydian, bright, sophisticated

### Major Thirteenth
- **Formula:** 1-3-5-7-9-13
- **Example:** C-E-G-B-D-A
- **Suffix:** Cmaj13
- **Character:** Full, rich, complex

## Dominant and Altered Chords

### Dominant Seventh
- **Formula:** 1-3-5-b7
- **Example:** C-E-G-Bb
- **Suffix:** C7

- **Character:** Suspended tension

- **Character:** Rich dominant

### Ninth, Suspended Fourth
- **Formula:** 1-4-5-b7-9
- **Example:** C-F-G-Bb-D
- **Suffix:** C9sus4
- **Character:** Suspended extended

- **Formula:** 1-5-b7-9-11

### Thirteenth
- **Formula:** 1-3-5-b7-9-13

- **Formula:** 1-b3-b5

- **Suffix:** C°
- **Character:** Maximum tension, unstable

## Interpreting Chords in Spectral Analysis

### Consonance Indicators
**Simple Chords (High Consonance):**
- Power chords (1-5): Very simple 3:2 ratio
- Major/minor triads: Simple integer ratios
- Should produce consonance scores > 30

**Complex Chords (Lower Consonance):**
- Extended harmonies (9ths, 11ths, 13ths): More frequencies competing
- Altered chords: Complex ratios
- Should produce consonance scores 15-30

**Dissonant Chords (Low Consonance):**
- Diminished: Tritone (45:32 ratio)
- Half-diminished: Multiple tense intervals
- Should produce consonance scores < 15

### Frequency Analysis of Chord Types

When analyzing spectral data:

**Major chord detected:**
- Clear peaks at root, major third, fifth
- Simple harmonic relationships
- Low spectral variance within chord

**Minor chord detected:**
- Clear peaks at root, minor third, fifth
- Slightly more complex than major
- Characteristic minor third ratio (6:5)

**Dominant seventh:**
- Four distinct peaks (1-3-5-b7)
- Added tritone creates tension
- Increased spectral complexity

**Extended harmonies:**
- Multiple frequency peaks
- Richer spectral density
- Higher overall harmonic complexity

### Tension and Resolution

**Musical Context:**
- Dominant (V7) → Tonic (I): Strong resolution
- Frequency analysis shows complex → simple transition
- Tension score should drop significantly at resolution

**Harmonic Progression:**
- Track consonance score over time
- Drops indicate tension (dominant, diminished)
- Rises indicate resolution (tonic, major)

This allows mathematical tracking of harmonic narrative through chord progressions.
